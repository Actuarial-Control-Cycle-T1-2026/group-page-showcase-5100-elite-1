### Executive Summary
Interstellar mining risk is out of this world, but Galaxy General Insurance Company (GGIC) 
brings it back down to Earth. Upon request, GGIC developed and offered a comprehensive
insurance package for Cosmic Quarry Mining Corporation (CQMC) which provided competitive 
coverage for interstellar mining whilst remaining profitable to GGIC. This report will cover the 
insurance product which covers four main hazard areas: business interruption, cargo loss, 
equipment failure and workers’ compensation. 
From the results of the actuarial modelling processes implemented, it was determined that 
although the uncertainty that arose from the interstellar mining industry could be extremely high 
due to the different risk characteristics of each solar system and the interstellar environments, the 
product was able to ensure comprehensive coverage of all these hazards whilst ensuring a 
positive net revenue, even at the worst 1% of cases. Some implemented design features included:

• Deductibles and limits to claims based on each solar system to protect from catastrophic losses.

• Exclusions for certain events that occur in specific hazards.

• Waiting periods to protect from fraudulent claims.

• Rating relativities applied to premiums to provide more tailored coverage of hazards.

• Discounts applied to incentivise safer interstellar mining practices.

The product was developed to be highly adaptable to future developments, with the models used 
to project future claims costs, premium returns and net revenue being able to be easily adjusted to 
capture new and emerging variables which could affect the frequency and severity of claims. 
There was also the possibility of adapting the product to extend coverage into other solar systems, 
should CQMC decide to expand the business operations into these yet to be insured regions.
CQMC can use the insurance package and integrate it into its Enterprise Risk Management 
frameworks in the following methods:

1) Risk Transfer – CQMC could transfer their extreme risks, such as exposure to strong solar 
radiation, to GGIC
2) Operational Insights – Managerial roles could incorporate GGIC’s sensor data into their 
safety dashboards to assist in detecting hazards in real-time
3) Capital Efficiency – Since GGIC would protect against large, unpredictable losses, 
CQMC could refocus its capital on smaller, routine risks
4) Incentive Alignment – The discount system utilised in GGIC’s product would align to 
CQMC’s safety goals, unifying both companies in their approach to managing risk.

### 2 Product Design
This product was designed to ensure the effective coverage of four hazard areas encountered by 
CQMC: business interruption, cargo loss, equipment failure, and worker’s compensation. Short-term (1 year) and long-term (10 year) considerations were made to ensure the sustainability of the product, the short- and long-term profitability, and the competitiveness of the insurance product compared to the market.

### 2.1 Business Interruption
GGIC defines business interruption as the loss of gross profit or contribution margins as well as 
additional expenses incurred by a company due to a disruption in their operations. Specifically, 
the disruption to the operations needed to be caused by direct physical damage to the insured’s 
property. As a result, all business interruption claims would require the following pieces of 
evidence to begin the approval process for a claim:

1) Incident and/or engineering reports which recorded and confirmed damages and loss to 
property
2) Operational logs reporting downtime and recovery
3) Third-party outage information to determine the level of dependencies of other operations 
on the damaged property in question

The premium would be paid annually, providing coverage for a term of one year including a 7-
day waiting period. A single claim would have a cover limit, where the excess would be retained 
by the policyholder. The total annual claim amount for a policyholder was also limited to a 
maximum amount. It was noted that data about the Bayesia System and Oryn Delta was not 
readily available. Thus, the premiums and claim limits for these systems were matched with 
Epsilon and Zeta respectively due to the similarity in the systems’ risk profiles, and the premiums 
were inflated by 20% to account for the additional uncertainty. Figures for premiums and claim 
limits are provided for each solar system in the table below (all monetary values are measured in 
Đ).

The severity of claims that were approved would be measured with reference to an agreed 
operational benchmark, depending on the extent of available data the insured could provide (see

Claims would also have an indemnity period, providing a maximum of 12 months coverage for 
each single annual policy purchased.
Exclusions for business interruptions claims included losses from:

• Economic downturn

• Planned maintenance, voluntary shutdown, or operational optimisation processes

• Property depreciation and degradation

• Cyber-attacks and wars/terrorism that are out of scope, unless included by endorsement

• Region-wide events which affect the safety of operations such as radiation spikes in the Bayesia system

• Supply chain impacts beyond the defined scope of dependencies

As more data becomes available, especially about the Bayesia System and Oryn Delta, premiums 
and limits would be adjusted to more accurately depict the risks faced by each solar system. It must be noted that, as a general guideline, premiums and limits should not exceed the figures than 
those in the Helionis cluster due to it being the system currently with the largest aggregate losses 
and volatility.

### 2.2 Cargo Loss
GGIC defines cargo loss as the partial or complete loss of cargo and/or its vessel. The premiums 
were set based on a rating system, with a base premium of Đ196,421.30. This rating system 
increased the premium of the policyholder by multiplying it by the associated risk relativity, 
summarised by the questions and responses below (see Appendix 8.3.2 for derivation of 
relativities).

• Do you ever transport gold or platinum? → Yes → ×44

• Do you ever travel on a route with risk level 4 or 5? → Yes → ×2

• Do you ever transport more than 75 tonnes? → Yes → ×1.75

• Do you ever transport more than Đ90 million worth of cargo? → Yes → ×1.25

• Did you have any claims last year? → No → ×0.9

The premiums were time dependent, with a discounting factor applied for policyholders with zero 
claims in the prior year. This acted as incentive for companies to purchase GGIC’s product and 
promoted safer mining and cargo transportation practices across all solar systems.
Claims would be paid up to a limit of Đ22.5 million, with the excess amount being transferred to 
the reinsurer, due to the extreme variances observed in aggregate severities. Furthermore, since 
different solar systems presented different risks, a list of deductibles was made to limit the 
extreme, catastrophic losses faced by the company. In the case that a route was taken through 
multiple solar systems, the deductibles were applied cumulatively. The deductible list is 
summarised in the table below.

### 2.3 Equipment Failure
GGIC defines equipment failure as the sudden mechanical or electrical breakdown of mining 
assets. The base premium for the coverage of a standard unit of equipment was Đ15,000. Similar 
to cargo loss, a rating system was used to adjust the premium using risk relativities, summarised 
by the questions and responses below (see details for premium and relativity setting in Appendix 
8.3.3).

• Is this piece of equipment an Ion Pulveriser? → Yes → ×1.3

• Is this piece of equipment a Flux Rider? → Yes → ×1.15

• Does the age of the piece of equipment exceed 5 years? → Yes → ×1.2

• Did you have any claims last year? → No → ×0.95

The product would cover the cost of repairs or full replacement of equipment, with a claim limit 
of Đ50 million as 4.8% of losses observed were extremely large. The reinsurer would cover 
claims in excess of this limit. In the Helionis Cluster specifically, Flux Riders were provided a 
higher limit due to their larger expected replacement cost which exceeded Đ25,000.

A claim would begin when a machine suffered a sudden and unexpected failure. As a result, 
GGIC would implement their fusion-powered risk sensors to monitor all insured equipment, with 
coverage automatically triggering upon detection of irreversible physical damage. This 
technology would drastically improve claims processing efficiency (360iResearch 2025; Munich 
Re 2024).

Different solar systems posed different risks and, as a result, deductibles were used to limit 
exposure to catastrophic losses. The policyholder would be required to pay the deductible prior to 
GGIC covering the remainder of the claim. This product would also exclude certain types of 
damage to ensure affordability for GGIC (see Appendix 8.3.3 for deductible/exclusion rationale).

This product would not cover failures caused by intentional neglect as well as losses from cyberattacks and unauthorised software changes, reflecting standard exclusions observed from Earth’s

The performance of the product would be reviewed yearly, with the goal of updating the relevant 
models as more data is obtained. This would allow the product to remain consistently fair across 
time as new mining technologies emerge. The product also offered a “maintenance credit” to 
reward policyholders with a 5% premium discount for using advanced predictive systems, which 
would improve safe mining practices across all solar systems (Munich Re 2024; Swiss Re 
Institute 2024).

### 2.4 Worker’s Compensation
Worker’s compensation was structured on an annual basis to provide funding for medical care, 
rehabilitation, and wage replacement for workers who were injured on the job or contracted 
work-related illnesses. This product also paid survivor benefits to the families of workers who 
died from work-related injuries or illnesses (National Academy of Social Insurance, 2024). No 
deductibles or limits were applied for medical expenses, with claim payments being made 
directly to medical institutions. For wage replacement and disability benefits, 70% of the salary 
would be paid weekly until the worker became rehabilitated or retired. For the death benefit, the 
insurance covered funeral expenses and dependent benefits (see Appendix 8.3.4 for details).

The annual premiums for each solar system were set in accordance with the level of risk exposure

### 3 Pricing and Capital Modelling
The four hazard areas were modelled with the assumption that they did not have any interactions 
between them. As a result, the pricing and capital modelling for the entire product would be 
equivalent to the sum of the premiums set for the product price and the sum of the required 
capital for reserving (EIOPA, 2026; International Association of Insurance Supervisors, 2023).

### 3.2 Cargo Loss
Premiums were determined at an annual level and hence the level of exposure needed to be 
accounted for in the distribution of claim frequencies. As mentioned earlier, the lower exposures 
inflated the number of claims. As a result, the annual claim frequency was modelled using a 
Negative Binomial Generalised Linear Model (GLM), with the addition of an offset term to 
account for the reduced exposures in the data to account for the riskier policyholders (Yan et al., 
2009). The claim severities were modelled using a Gamma GLM with a log link. The models, in 
addition to some select characteristics, were used to set the base premium (see Appendix 8.4.2.1 
for detailed calculations).
Due to the nature of cargo transportation possibly facing the risks of multiple solar systems, a 
summary of short-term and long-term aggregate claims, premiums and net revenue was made on 
the entire portfolio rather than each distinct solar system. The table below summarises the 
distributions, using a Monte Carlo simulation with N = 10000.

The results showed that even in the worst 1% of cases, the net revenue achieved by GGIC for 
cargo loss coverage was still profitable. Furthermore, the underwriting combined ratio was 
calculated to be approximately 84–86% in both the short- and long-term, proving that the benefit

Stress testing was conducted by imposing extreme values on characteristics which were included 
in models and subject to change. These variables were route risk and debris density, specifically 
the cases where: 1) all routes were faced with maximum risk, 2) debris density was maximal, and 
3) both 1 and 2 were in force. In the first two cases, the company net revenue was severely 
reduced but remained solvent but, in the most extreme circumstances in case 3, the company

as both the expected net revenue and 99% TVaR were positive (see Appendix 8.4.2.2 for full 
information on stress test distributions).

Solar radiation was identified as a potential hazard across all solar systems. This was not included 
as a covariate in either model but must be considered for catastrophic event stress tests. 
Catastrophic radiation events may be partially reflected through the route risk stress tests, but the 
approach taken was still limited in its modelling capabilities.

& Mosher, 2018). The aggregate loss distribution was estimated using a Monte-Carlo simulation 
100,000 times (see Appendix 8.4.4.1 for details).

faced in each relevant solar system. While the 
Bayesia System displayed relatively moderate 
risks, the Helionis Cluster and Oryn Delta

to the asteroid belt and gravitational fluctuations

The best scenarios for GGIC’s product involved 
attritional claims with low claim frequencies and 
close to relatively negligible severity. These scenarios would result in stable annual figures. 
Moderate risk scenarios involved localised, multi-week shut down periods, with medium to high 
levels of claim frequencies and amounts from some solar systems, but not portfolio wide. This 
scenario would lead to an increase in annual losses. In the most catastrophic cases where events 
affected multiple solar systems at a time, singular events would trigger high claim frequencies as 
well as severities due to the heavy right-tailed nature of these claims. A large jump in annual 
aggregate losses would be observed, with a slow recovery for all related businesses.
As a result, the key risks could be categorised into risks that affected: 1) operations, 2) risk event 
frequencies and 3) key business functions which affected other aspects of business. These risks 
were ranked from most severe to least:
1) Operational severity – the potential length of downtime
2) Risk event frequency – how often the risky conditions occur
3) Aggregation potential – how dependent other areas of business are affected by the risk

Excessive radiation and wind levels were considered the riskiest factor, affecting all the three 
categories of risk. It would cause disruptions to navigational, operational and communication 
systems as well as the safety of employees and increase the frequency of risk events. 
Gravitational fluctuations behaved similarly to excess radiation levels but had a less detrimental 
effect in comparison. The activity of the asteroid belt(s) in a system would affect the frequency of 
risk events, mainly affecting the cargo loss and business interruption hazard areas. Extreme 
planetary climates were considered to generally not be too risky, mainly affecting employees and 
potentially business operations only on a planetary scale. Low visibility was considered the least 
risky factor, only significantly affecting cargo loss.

### 6 Data and Data Limitations
### 6.1 Data Sources
The project data provided the primary foundation for this analysis. Financial statements were 
examined from similar high-risk industries. This research established a realistic cost of capital 
rate and expense ratio. These benchmarks were used to ensure premium prices remained 
competitive in the short- and long-terms.

### 6.2 Data Limitations
• Incorrect or missing data reduced the accuracy of modelling and revenue projections. This 
may have led to some variables lacking statistical significance during the testing of regression 
models. The data also lacked deeper levels of granularity, limiting the ability to tailor the 
product to the needs of the client. Some hazards also lacked clear mappings to specific solar 
systems.

• Limited historical information about inflation and spot rates proved difficult to model 
realistically with any distribution.

• The absence of prior data or models about interstellar mining meant there were no 
comparative benchmarks to evaluate the effectiveness of the product.

• Limited information was known about the finances of GGIC. As a result, assumptions were 
made about their financial structure and expenditure.

### 6.3 Use of Artificial Intelligence (AI)
Large Language Models (LLMs) were used to assist with technical components of the report. The 
LLMs were utilised in three main ways:
1) Optimising code to increase efficiency and reduce computational complexity.
2) Transforming qualitative risk characteristics into usable data for the modelling process.
3) Editorial suggestions to code and writing to ensure correctness and coherence.

Responses provided by LLMs were used carefully, ensuring that the outputs received from these 
AI tools were audited manually throughout the development of the product. This ensured that 
human judgement was consistently throughout the project.

### 7 Conclusion
This report proposes a comprehensive insurance package for Cosmic Quarry Mining Corporation 
(CQMC), covering the four major hazard areas of business interruption, cargo loss, equipment 
failure, and workers’ compensation. Each product was tailored to the operational and 
environmental conditions across different solar systems. Actuarial tools, including generalised 
linear models and Monte Carlo simulations, were used to estimate losses, premiums and capital 
requirements. The results indicated that the portfolio was expected to remain profitable under 
normal conditions. Stress testing showed that policy limits, deductibles, exclusions and 
reinsurance helped control extreme risks making the proposed insurance framework both 
sustainable and adaptable for future space mining operations.
